# Preparing Front/Back Matter: Maps, Introductions, and Appendices
**Duration:** 60 minutes
**Level:** Experienced Translator
**Goal:** Prepare essential supplementary materials including map names, introductions, and appendices for publication
## Introduction
**Time:** 5 minutes
Your translation of the biblical text is complete. But a published Scripture isn't just the translated text—it needs supporting materials that help readers engage with the content effectively.
Maps help readers visualize the geography of biblical events. An introduction orients readers to what they're about to read and why it matters. Appendices provide additional resources like glossaries, chronologies, or explanatory notes.
These "back matter" elements are often overlooked until the typesetter asks for them. This module will guide you through preparing these essential supplementary materials.
### What This Module Covers
- Preparing map names using the Map Labeler Plugin
- Drafting an introduction to the New Testament or Bible
- Creating appendices and supplementary materials
- Working with typesetters to determine format requirements
### Prerequisites
Before starting this module, ensure:
- Your translation text is finalized
- You've established contact with your typesetter
- You have editing permissions for all books including the special books (INT, XXA, etc.) in your Paratext project
- You understand basic USFM markers (or have reference materials available)
## Procedure Walkthrough
**Time:** 40 minutes
### Understanding Back Matter Components
Back matter includes several types of supplementary content:
**Maps:** Visual representations of biblical geography with place names in your language 
**Introduction:** Explains what the New Testament/Bible is, why it matters, and how to read it **Appendices:** Additional helpful materials (glossaries, weights and measures, chronologies, etc.)
Each serves a different purpose, but all help readers engage more fully with the Scripture text.
### Part 1: Preparing Map Names
Maps in published Scripture help readers understand the geographical context of biblical events. Place names — Jerusalem, Galilee, Jericho, Rome — need to appear on those maps in your language, spelled consistently with how they appear in the translated text. The Paratext Diagram Labeler plugin is designed to make this possible.
**How it works**
The plugin reads your project's Biblical Terms renderings and uses them to propose labels for each place on a map. Your job is to review those proposals, make corrections where needed, and ensure that what appears on the map matches what appears in the text. The plugin stores all your labels in a Label Dictionary, so once you've confirmed a place name it stays consistent across all maps in your project.
When you save your work, the labels are exported automatically into files that your typesetter can use directly in their mapping software.
**Before you begin**
- Paratext 9 should already be installed
- It is strongly recommended that you use the Biblical Terms tool to guess renderings for all Names before opening the Labeler — this gives the plugin the best starting point for proposals
- Check with your typesetter which file format they need (.mapx for Map Creator, or .idml for Adobe InDesign)
**Getting started**
1. Install the Paratext Diagram Labeler plugin if not already installed — download from [software.sil.org/labeler](https://software.sil.org/labeler)
2. Work through your maps using the plugin, reviewing and confirming place name labels
3. Save your work — labels are exported automatically to your project's shared folder and included in Send/Receive
4. Notify your typesetter that labels are ready
**For detailed guidance**, refer to the full user guide at [software.sil.org/labeler/learn](https://software.sil.org/labeler/learn), which covers installation, the interface, working with labels, and exporting finished maps.
⚠️ *Note: This plugin is currently in beta. If you encounter problems or need help installing it, contact your Language Technology Consultant.*
### Part 2: Drafting an Introduction to the NT/Bible
An introduction helps readers understand what they're about to read. It's especially important for readers encountering Scripture for the first time.
**Purpose of the Introduction:**
- Explains what the New Testament/Bible is
- Provides historical and cultural context
- Helps readers understand how to approach the text
- May explain translation decisions or text organization

1. In Paratext, navigate to the book **INT** (Introduction)
	1. This is a special book code specifically for introductory material
	2. If you don't see INT in your book list, you may need to enable it in project settings
2. Ensure the book has a **\\h** (header) line
	1. This line identifies the book
	2. Example: \\h Introduction to the New Testament
### Step 2: Structure Your Introduction
Use these USFM markers to format your introduction properly:
💡 **Tip:** If you're new to USFM markers, think of them like formatting codes. \\mt1 = Main Title (level 1), \\is = Introduction Section heading, \\ip = Introduction Paragraph. Each marker tells the typesetter how to format that content.
**\\mt1** - Main Title
- Use for the introduction title
- Example: \\mt1 Introduction to the New Testament
**\\is** - Introduction Section Heading
- Use for section headings within your introduction
- Example: \\is What is the New Testament?
- Example: \\is How to Read This Book
**\\ip** - Introduction Paragraph
- Use for body text paragraphs
- Most of your content will use this marker
### Step 3: Content to Include
Consider including sections on:
**What is the New Testament/Bible?**
- Basic explanation of what Scripture is
- How it was written and preserved
- Its significance for faith and life
**The Structure of the New Testament**
- Brief overview of the four Gospels
- Acts and the early church
- Paul's letters and general epistles
- Revelation
**How to Read the New Testament**
- Guidance for first-time readers
- Understanding different types of literature (narrative, letters, prophecy)
- Encouragement and practical tips
**About This Translation**
- Brief note about your translation work
- Any important translation decisions readers should know
- How to use cross-references, footnotes, and other helps
**Tips for Writing a Good Introduction:**
- Write in clear, accessible language
- Keep it relatively brief (2-4 pages typically)
- Focus on helping readers, not impressing scholars
- Have it reviewed by community advisors and your Translation Consultant
- Consider your readers' familiarity with Scripture
✅ **Best Practice:** Read introductions from published Bibles in related languages for inspiration, but write in your own voice for your specific audience. What works for one culture may not work for another.
### Step 4: Example Structure
Here's a sample structure using proper markers:
\\h Introduction to the New Testament
\\mt1 Introduction to the New Testament
\\is What is the New Testament?
\\ip The New Testament is the second part of the Christian Bible. It tells the story of Jesus Christ and the early church...
\\is The Four Gospels
\\ip The first four books—Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John—tell the story of Jesus' life, teaching, death, and resurrection...
\\is How to Read This Book
\\ip You can start reading anywhere, but many people begin with the Gospel of Mark...

### Part 3: Creating Appendices
Appendices provide additional helpful resources for readers. Common appendices include:
**Glossary:** Definitions of difficult or important terms **Weights and Measures:** Explanations of biblical measurements **Chronology:** Timeline of biblical events **Maps:** (In addition to maps in the text) **Names Index:** Alphabetical list of important names **Topical Index:** Key topics and where to find them
### Choosing Appendix Content
**Not every publication needs every type of appendix.** Consider:
- What will help your readers most?
- What's your page budget?
- What's culturally appropriate and useful?
- What does your typesetter recommend?
**Consult with your Translation Consultant and typesetter** about which appendices to include.
### Where to Create Appendices in Paratext
Paratext provides several book codes for appendices:
**General Appendix Books:**
- **XXA** - Appendix A
- **XXB** - Appendix B
- **XXC** - Appendix C
**Specific Purpose Books:**
- **BAK** - Back Matter
- **OTH** - Other Matter
- **TDX** - Topical Index
- **NDX** - Names Index
**Important:** Check with your typesetter about which book codes to use. Different publishing systems may have different preferences.
⚠️ **Important:** Always coordinate with your typesetter BEFORE creating extensive appendix content. Nothing is more frustrating than spending hours formatting an appendix only to learn the typesetter needs it in a completely different format or book code.
### Creating Appendix Content
1. Navigate to the appropriate appendix book (e.g., XXA)
2. Use standard USFM markers:
	1. **\\h** - Header line (book identification)
	2. **\\mt1** - Main title
	3. **\\s** - Section headings
	4. **\\p** - Paragraphs
	5. **\\li** - List items (for indexes)
3. Create your content following your typesetter's specifications
**Example Glossary Structure:**
\\h Glossary
\\mt1 Glossary of Important Terms
\\s A
\\p Atonement - The act of making amends for sin...
\\s B
\\p Baptism - A ceremony using water to show...
### Working with Your Typesetter
**Before creating extensive appendix content:**
1. Contact your typesetter
2. Ask which appendices they recommend
3. Get format specifications
4. Confirm which Paratext book codes to use
5. Ask about page limits or other constraints
This prevents wasted work if format requirements change.

## Troubleshooting
**Time:** 5 minutes
### Common Issues and Solutions
**Problem:** "I don't see the INT book in my project"
- **Solution:** The INT book may need to be added to your project. Go to Project \> Book Names and Abbreviations, and ensure INT is enabled. Contact your Language Technology Consultant if you need help.
**Problem:** "I'm not sure what USFM markers to use for my introduction"
- **Solution:** Use \\mt1 for titles, \\is for section headings, and \\ip for paragraphs. For more complex formatting, consult the USFM documentation or ask your Language Technology Consultant.
**Problem:** "My introduction seems too long/too short"
- **Solution:** Typical introductions are 2-4 pages. Focus on what readers need to know. Get feedback from your Translation Consultant on appropriate length.
**Problem:** "I don't know which appendices to include"
- **Solution:** Start by asking your typesetter what they recommend. Common choices are glossary and maps. Your Translation Consultant can also provide guidance based on similar projects.
**Problem:** "The typesetter is asking for content in a different format than what I created"
- **Solution:** This is why it's important to coordinate with the typesetter BEFORE creating extensive content. Ask for their specifications and reformat as needed. Your Language Technology Consultant can help with technical formatting issues.
**Problem:** "My appendix content isn't displaying properly in Paratext"
- **Solution:** Check that you're using the correct USFM markers. Make sure your book has a \\h line. Switch to Preview or Formatted view to see how it will appear. Contact your Language Technology Consultant for formatting help.
### When to Ask for Help
