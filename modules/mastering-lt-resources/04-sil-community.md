## Content

**Why should you use the two SIL Community websites ([community.software.sil.org](http://community.software.sil.org) and [community.scripture.software.sil.org](http://community.scripture.software.sil.org))?**

These two websites are essential hubs for language technology workers at SIL Global, and this module explains why each matters.

### 1. SIL Language Software Community

This is the broader SIL software community forum, covering the full suite of SIL's language technology tools. A worker should use it because:

- **Technical support & troubleshooting** — Get help from developers and experienced users on tools like Keyman, SIL fonts, FLEx (FieldWorks Language Explorer), Language Forge, and more.
- **Announcements & updates** — Stay current on new releases, features, and new recommendations across SIL's software ecosystem.
- **Peer collaboration** — Connect with linguists, developers, and field workers globally who face similar technical challenges.
- **Bug reporting** — A structured place to report issues and track resolutions.
- **Best practices** — Learn how others are solving real-world language documentation and localization problems.

### 2. SIL Scripture Software Community

This site is focused specifically on Bible translation and scripture-related technology tools. It's critical for workers involved in translation projects because:

- **Scripture App Builder, Reading App Builder** — Get guidance on building and publishing scripture apps for minority language communities.
- **Translation checking workflows** — Discuss consultant checking, back translation, and quality assurance processes.
- **Publisher & organization coordination** — Interact with United Bible Societies (UBS) partners and other translation organizations who co-develop these tools.
- **Training resources** — Access tutorials, webinars, and documentation shared by the community.

Together, they form SIL's **collective institutional knowledge base** — reducing duplicated effort, accelerating problem-solving, and connecting workers to a global community of practice rather than working in isolation.

The two SIL software community sites function as collaborative hubs for language and scripture technology. Built on the Discourse platform, these forums allow participants to engage in structured discussions organized by categories, topics, and tags. Users can interact with posts, create new discussions, and share information with others. They can manage their engagement through customizable tracking settings, which provide notifications for new activity based on personal interest. Additionally, the sites facilitate development by allowing members to request new features and vote on improvements. Because these communities are monitored by experts and developers, they offer a reliable environment for troubleshooting and professional support.

**Question:** You are reading a long topic on Discourse and want to jump back to where you left off. What feature helps you do this, and what visual cues tell you which topics are new, updated, or fully read?

- When you select a topic title, Discourse automatically returns you to your last read post. To identify the status of topics at a glance: new or updated content appears above a light red line, while fully read topics are shown with light gray titles. You can also use the timeline scroll bar to navigate within a topic.

The two community sites are: [SIL Language Software Community](https://community.software.sil.org/) and [SIL Scripture Software Community](https://community.scripture.software.sil.org/).

**Question:** A trainer just found a helpful post that solves a common problem her team keeps asking about. She wants to share the solution directly via email without having her team log in or search for it. What feature should she use, and will her team be able to view the content without an account?

- The trainer should use the **Share button**, which generates a direct link to the specific post. She can then copy that link into an email or message for her team. Since anyone can view the site without logging in, her team will be able to read the content without needing a registered account — an account is only required to create topics or post replies.

**Question:** You work with Keyman daily and also occasionally use Dictionary App Builder. You want to stay informed about both tools, but you don't want your inbox flooded with emails every time someone replies to a Dictionary App Builder thread. What tracking levels would you set for each tool, and how do you configure these settings?

- For **Keyman**, you would set the tracking level to **Watching**, since this notifies you of all new posts and topics — ideal for a tool you use every day. For **Dictionary App Builder**, you would set it to **Tracking**, which shows a visual count of new posts without sending email notifications, keeping your inbox manageable. To configure these settings:
  1. Click your **profile icon**.
  2. Go to **Preferences**.
  3. Find the **Tracking** section.
  4. Choose your category tracking levels for each tool.
  5. Click **Save Changes**.

**Question:** You are troubleshooting a formatting issue in Bloom and you find a long, detailed topic that looks promising. However, the thread has hundreds of replies and you're not sure where to start. What should you do first, and what tool can help you navigate through the conversation efficiently?

- You should start by reading the **original post (OP)**, which is the very first message in the thread. The OP is the spark that kicks off the entire discussion and sets the technical direction for all the replies that follow — making it the essential context you need before diving into the rest of the conversation. Once you understand the issue from the OP, you can use the **timeline scroll bar** on the side of the screen to navigate smoothly through the lengthy thread, allowing you to jump to relevant sections without having to scroll through every single reply manually.
