# Module 2: Interlinearizing Texts

**Estimated time:** 60 minutes

**Purpose:** A team hands you a text — a folk story transcription, a set of example sentences, an early literacy draft — and it's just raw text until someone breaks it into words, morphemes, and glosses. That work is what actually builds the project's lexicon and grammatical sketch. This module prepares you to do that work yourself, and to help a team member do it too.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this module you will be able to:

- Add a text to a project's baseline and enter basic information about it.
- Distinguish what the Gloss tab and the Analyze tab each show, and know when to use each.
- Interlinearize a sentence: approve correct suggestions, create new lexical entries, and split a word into its morphemes.
- Combine words into a single analyzable unit when they function as one phrase.
- Use keyboard shortcuts to move through a text efficiently.
- Add a free translation and a note to a sentence.

## Connect

**✏️ Reflection.** Think of a time you had to explain the grammar of a sentence to someone — a student, a colleague, yourself trying to work something out — without first breaking the sentence into its parts.

- How far did you get before you had to stop and separate out the individual words and pieces?
- Now imagine doing that for an entire text, word by word, and having software remember every decision you made so it can suggest the same analysis the next time a similar word shows up. What would that save you, over doing it from scratch every time?

That's what interlinearizing does: it turns a block of raw text into an analyzed, searchable structure — one word, one morpheme, one gloss at a time.

## Content

**From baseline to analysis.** In Module 1 you set up a project with a vernacular and analysis writing system. Now you'll bring a real text into it. In the **Texts & Words** area, the **Interlinear Texts** view has a **Baseline** tab where you paste or type in the raw text, and an **Info** tab where you record basic details like the title and genre.

Once a text is in the baseline, FLEx gives you two different views for working on it:

- The **Gloss** tab shows each word as a whole unit — you're just assigning it a gloss.
- The **Analyze** tab shows each word broken into its morphemes — you're assigning glosses and categories at the morpheme level, not just the whole-word level.

A word marked **\*\*\*** hasn't been interlinearized yet. Click into a word to open its **focus box**, where you actually enter or adjust the interlinearization. Some suggestions appear in a different color — those are exact matches already in the lexicon, or words you've interlinearized the same way before.

**Approving, creating, and splitting.** For each word, you're doing one of a few things:

- If the suggested analysis is correct, **approve** it (green checkmark, or **Enter**) and move on.
- If the word isn't in the lexicon yet, **create a new entry** for it and fill in the gloss.
- If the word is made of more than one morpheme — for example *walked* is *walk* + *-ed* — **split it**, add the root as a new entry, and confirm the affix's gloss (a common affix like *-ed* may already be pre-filled if it's already in the lexicon).

**NOTE** A word can also be a **variant** of an existing entry rather than its own separate lexical entry — for example, an irregular form like *said* is a variant of *say*, not a new headword in its own right. Marking it as a variant keeps your lexicon from filling up with duplicate entries for the same underlying word.

**Combining words into one unit.** Some multi-word sequences function as a single grammatical unit — a compound preposition, a fixed phrase, a contraction spread across two written words. Select the words and use the **link/combine** function to treat them as one analyzable unit, then gloss and categorize that combined unit as a whole.

**WARNING** FLEx's suggestions aren't always right, especially for ambiguous words. A word with more than one common sense (a financial institution vs. the edge of a river, for example) may get suggested with the wrong sense — click the down arrow beside the Lex. Entries line and choose the correct sense before approving.

**Moving through a text efficiently.** You don't have to interlinearize a text top-to-bottom in one pass — click any word to jump straight to it. These shortcuts speed up the repetitive parts:

- **Tab** — move to the next item within the current word.
- **Enter** — approve the current suggestion and move to the next word.
- **Shift+Enter** — move to the next word *without* approving.
- **Ctrl+J** — approve and jump to the next word that's still incomplete.
- **Ctrl+S** — approve and stay on the current word.
- **Ctrl+M** — combine the current word with the next one into a phrase.

**Free translations and notes.** Beyond the word-by-word analysis, each sentence has a **Free** textbox for a natural, idiomatic translation of the whole sentence, and a **Note** icon (in the toolbar) for attaching a note — useful for recording something a translator told you about a sentence that doesn't fit anywhere else.

**Reviewing your work.** Rather than trying to recall every word or entry you've touched, use **View → Texts & Words → Word List Concordance** — it lists every wordform in your text alongside its current gloss, which is a much faster way to review or spot-check your interlinearization than scrolling back through the text itself.

**Key takeaways**

- Baseline text goes in via the Interlinear Texts view; basic info (title, genre) goes on the Info tab.
- Gloss = whole-word assignment; Analyze = morpheme-level breakdown.
- For each word: approve what's correct, create new entries for what isn't in the lexicon yet, split words into morphemes when needed, and mark irregular forms as variants rather than new entries.
- Combine multi-word units with the link function; correct wrong-sense suggestions using the down arrow beside Lex. Entries.
- Keyboard shortcuts (Tab, Enter, Shift+Enter, Ctrl+J, Ctrl+S, Ctrl+M) make moving through a text much faster once you're comfortable with the workflow.
- Word List Concordance gives you a fast overview of every wordform and gloss in a text, useful for review.

## Challenge

### Exercise 2.1 — Interlinearize a real passage

**The situation.** A team member has handed you a short passage — three or four sentences, in the language they're documenting — and asked you to get it properly into FLEx so it's actually analyzed, not just typed in as a blob of text.

**Bring in the text:**

1. Open the test project you created in Module 1 (or create a new one if you'd rather start fresh).
2. In the Texts & Words area, go to Interlinear Texts and select the Baseline tab.
3. Type or paste in a short passage — three or four sentences — in your vernacular language. If you don't have field data handy, write a few simple sentences yourself; the point is to practice the workflow, not to produce publishable analysis.
4. On the Info tab, give the text a title.

**Interlinearize it:**

1. Switch to the Analyze tab and work through the text word by word: approve what's correct, create new lexical entries for words that aren't in the lexicon yet, and split any word that's made of more than one morpheme.
2. If you encounter a multi-word sequence that functions as one unit, combine it using the link function (or **Ctrl+M**).
3. If FLEx suggests the wrong sense for an ambiguous word, click the down arrow beside the Lex. Entries line and choose the correct sense before approving.
4. Practice moving through the text using the keyboard shortcuts above rather than clicking every time.

**Add the finishing touches:**

1. In the Free textbox for at least one sentence, enter a natural translation of the whole sentence.
2. Add a note to at least one sentence using the Note icon.

**Review what you produced:**

1. Go to **View → Texts & Words → Word List Concordance** to see every wordform in your text listed alongside its gloss — a much faster way to review your work than trying to recall each entry from memory.

**✏️ Produce this (a mentor will review it).** Using the Word List Concordance list, pick one wordform you split into morphemes and write 1–2 sentences explaining why you split it the way you did (or why you left another wordform as a single unanalyzed entry instead).

## Change

**Self-assessment — can you explain it to a colleague?**

1. What's the difference between what the Gloss tab shows you and what the Analyze tab shows you?
2. If FLEx already suggests the fully correct analysis for a word, what's the fastest way to accept it and move to the next word?
3. When would you combine two words into a single analyzable unit rather than interlinearizing them separately?

*You should be able to say:* (1) Gloss shows each word as a whole unit with a single gloss; Analyze breaks the word down into its morphemes, each with its own gloss and category. (2) Press Enter (or click the green checkmark) to approve and advance — no need to retype anything FLEx already got right. (3) When the words function together as a single grammatical unit — a compound preposition, a fixed phrase, a contraction — rather than as independent words that each carry their own separate analysis.

**✏️ Take it to your context.** Think of a real text — even a few sentences — that a team you support could hand you next. What do you expect will be the hardest part of interlinearizing it: unfamiliar vocabulary, ambiguous words, or morphology you haven't seen before? Naming it now means you'll know what to ask for help with.

**Next step.** In Module 3 you'll put the parser to work: once you've interlinearized several sentences by hand, FLEx can start suggesting analyses automatically based on the patterns you've already taught it — and you'll use the Word Analyses and Word List Concordance views to review and correct suggestions across a whole text at once, then generate a text chart to see discourse-level patterns.
