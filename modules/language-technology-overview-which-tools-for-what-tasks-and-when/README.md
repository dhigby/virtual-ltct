# Language Technology Overview
## Course Overview
**Target Audience:** Language Technology Consultants (LTCs) and specialists supporting Bible translation and language development teams
**Duration:** Approximately 3-4 hours (self-paced)
**Learning Objectives:**
1. Identify key software tools used for Bible translation and language development
2. Match specific language development and translation tasks with appropriate software
**Assessment:** End-of-module quizzes with a final comprehensive assessment

##  Module 1: Introduction Review -done {toggle="true"}
	### What's Working Well
	**This is a strong opening module.** The Connect prompt is one of the better ones in the course — "Think about a time when you helped someone choose the right tool for a job" is universal enough that any learner can engage with it, regardless of experience level. It also sets up the course's central theme elegantly.
	**The scope is appropriately restrained.** 15-minute Content covering one workflow, one scenario in Challenge, a tight Change section. This is the right level of complexity for a first module — it builds confidence before the tool-heavy modules arrive.
	**The scenario is perfect for Module 1.** It's simple, realistic, and has an unambiguous answer that directly reinforces the lesson's key insight. Learners leave with an immediate, applicable takeaway.
	---
	### Priority Issues
	**The Content section is missing a Key Takeaways block**, though given the module is only 15 minutes of content covering a single framework, this is less critical here than elsewhere.
	**The Change section has no transfer prompt or forward link.** "What questions do you still have?" is a reasonable closing question, but for a first module it's a missed opportunity to build anticipation for what's coming. A single "Looking Ahead" line — even just noting that the next module dives into the specific tools for word collection and phonological analysis — would help learners see the course arc from the start.
	**Minor wording note:** "Written for outsiders (linguists, academics)" describing the phonology statement is slightly awkward — linguists and academics *are* the outsiders in this context, but that phrasing might read oddly to learners who identify as linguists themselves. Something like "Written for a technical linguistic audience" might land more cleanly.
	**Timing looks right** — this is one of the few modules that fits the framework comfortably.
	---
	Overall, Module 1 does its job well. It's focused, appropriately scoped, and sets a good tone. The main opportunity is a stronger Close that connects forward into the rest of the course.

This is exactly how a Connect should work.

	**The troubleshooting scenario is excellent.** It's the right level of complexity for Module 2, it has a satisfying answer (no, you don't need to start over — WeSay exports to LIFT which FLEx reads), and it reinforces the data flow concept that Module 9 later synthesises. Good longitudinal design.

The third question — "How would you explain to a community why they might need different tools at different stages?" — is actually a strong question, but it's more of a Challenge-level task than a Change reflection. The Change section would be stronger with one consolidating insight and one forward-looking prompt.
	**Timing looks appropriate** — one of the well-scoped modules.
	---
	Overall Module 2 is one of the stronger modules in the course, sitting alongside Modules 1 and 9. The conceptual framing before the tool list is a practice your colleague should be encouraged to apply consistently across the modules that lead with raw tool lists.

## Module 4: Literacy tools -done {toggle="true"}
	## Review of the Literacy Tools Lesson
	### What's Working Well
	The lesson fits the 4C framework (Connect, Content, Challenge, Change) sensibly. The **Challenge section is the strongest part** — it gives learners three distinct, concrete scenarios that build on each other rather than being variations of the same question. The troubleshooting scenario in particular is excellent: it requires learners to understand *how* a tool works, not just *what* it does. That's a higher-order thinking demand, which is appropriate.
	The **Content section also does a good job** of grouping tools by function (primer development vs. literacy materials creation), which gives learners a mental model to hang information on rather than presenting a flat list.
	---
	### Learning Design & Structure
	**Timing is misaligned with the framework.** The skill spec calls for Connect at \~10 minutes, but this lesson allocates only 5. More significantly, the Change section is listed as 10 minutes but contains only four reflective questions with no transfer activity, action plan, or "Looking Ahead" element — which means it will likely compress into 3–4 minutes in practice, or feel unresolved.
	**The Content section is structured as a reference list, not instruction.** There's a video ("Play the video Literacy Tools") but no framing before or debriefing after it. Learners are dropped into bullet-point tool descriptions without knowing *why* they're learning this order or *how* these tools relate to each other. A brief conceptual map — even a sentence like "these tools fall into two workflows: primer development and materials creation" — would significantly help.
	**There's no Key Takeaways section at the end of Content**, which the framework explicitly requires. This is a meaningful gap — it's where consolidation happens before learners move into Challenge.
	---
	### Content Accuracy & Depth
	The tool descriptions are accurate at a functional level, but **several are quite thin**. For example, PrimerPrep is described as "easier to use but less powerful" and "good for teams wanting simpler workflow" — but a learner can't make a real tool choice with that alone. What makes it less powerful? When would you actually choose it over PrimerPro?
	**The publishing workflow diagram is valuable** but could use one sentence of explanation — what does "Desktop publishing (MS Publisher)" actually add to the output that the primer tool doesn't produce directly?
	One thing worth raising with your colleague: **is Bloom truly the "primary" literacy tool**, or is that framing relative to a specific organisational context? A learner new to the field might wonder what makes it primary versus, say, Reading App Builder for a smartphone-heavy community. Given that the Challenge scenario describes a community where "most adults have smartphones," this framing could create mild cognitive dissonance.
	---
	### Clarity & Language
	The lesson reads clearly overall. The bullet-point format is consistent and scannable. A few small observations:
	- "Alpha Tiles — Features continually added" is vague. Is that a selling point or a caveat? It could read as "the tool is still under development," which might concern a practitioner.
	- The Connect prompt — "Have you seen situations where translation outpaced literacy development? Or vice versa? What happened?" — is a strong question, but it assumes learners have field experience. If some learners are newer practitioners, a follow-up like "If not, what would you imagine the consequences might be?" would make it inclusive.
	---
	### Practical Applicability
	The **Challenge scenario is realistic and well-scoped** — a new orthography, limited lexicon, mixed preferences, smartphones. That's the kind of constrained context practitioners actually face.
	However, **question 4 in the Challenge ("What's the relationship between primer development and lexicon building?") is somewhat orphaned**. The Content section doesn't explicitly address this relationship, so learners are being asked to synthesise something they may not have been taught. Either the Content section needs a paragraph on this, or the question needs a hint/scaffold.
	The **Change section's reflective questions are good** but would benefit from at least one forward-looking prompt — something like "What's one thing you'd do differently in your current or upcoming project based on today's session?" Without a transfer prompt, the section closes the session without anchoring learning to action.
	---
	### Summary of Priority Suggestions
	In rough order of impact:
	1. Add a **Key Takeaways** section at the end of Content — this is a framework requirement and a learning need.
	2. Add a **video debrief or framing** around the Content video — don't let it stand alone.
	3. Add a **transfer/action prompt** to Change so the session closes with intention.
	4. Either **teach the primer/lexicon relationship** in Content or **scaffold it** in the Challenge question.
	5. Expand the **PrimerPrep description** to give learners enough to actually choose between tools.
	6. Consider a **"Looking Ahead"** element at the end if this is part of a multi-session course.
	Overall, this is a solid draft with clear bones. The Challenge section in particular shows real pedagogical care. The main gap is that the Content section functions more as a reference sheet than a teaching sequence — bridging that would lift the whole lesson considerably.
## Module 5: Scripture Translation, Publishing & Research -done {toggle="true"}
	## Review of the Scripture Translation Tools Lesson
	### What's Working Well
	This is a **more content-rich lesson than the previous one**, and the tool descriptions are noticeably stronger — most include enough "why" to help learners make real decisions, not just recognise names. The **Common workflow note** ("Create first draft in Adapt It → Refine in Paratext") is exactly the kind of integrative thinking that's often missing from tool-survey lessons. More of that connective tissue would lift the whole lesson.
	The **Challenge section is again the strongest part**. Seven tool-selection scenarios is ambitious, but they're meaningfully distinct — each one isolates a different decision variable (collaboration, mobility, oral preference, publication stage). The two troubleshooting scenarios are also well-chosen, particularly the USFM one, which requires learners to think about data migration, not just tool features.
	---
	### Learning Design & Structure
	**The timing is significantly over-budget.** Content is allocated 40 minutes and Challenge 30 minutes — that's 70 minutes before Connect and Change, putting the session at roughly 85+ minutes total. The framework targets 60 minutes. Either some content needs to move to a companion reference document, or this should be split into two sessions: one on translation/checking tools, one on publishing/oral tools.
	**The Connect section is functional but generic.** The three prompts are reasonable, but they're interchangeable with the opener for almost any software training session. A more grounded prompt — something that surfaces *what's at stake* in tool choice specifically for Bible translation ("Have you seen a team lose months of work, or produce an inconsistent translation, because of poor tooling decisions?") — would create stronger motivation for the Content that follows.
	**There's still no Key Takeaways section** at the end of Content, and given the volume of tools covered here (15+), this is even more important than in the previous lesson. Without consolidation, learners move into Challenge carrying a lot of unorganised information.
	**The Change section has a structural problem.** It contains both a "Self-Assessment" block and a "Reflection Questions" block — that's effectively two Change sections. Self-assessment questions tend to be yes/no confidence checks ("Can you explain X?"), while the reflection questions are more generative. In practice these will blur together. A cleaner structure would be: 3–4 key reflection questions, then one forward-looking transfer prompt.
	---
	### Content Accuracy & Depth
	**There's a formatting error in the Paratext Lite section** that looks like a copy-paste artifact:
	> *Paratext Lite - Mobile companion*
		- Runs on Android tablets/phones<br>PTXPrint- Doesn't have full Paratext capabilities
	"PTXPrint- Doesn't have full Paratext capabilities" appears to be a stray line that got merged into the wrong tool description. PTXPrint is its own tool covered later — this line likely belongs as a clarifying note about Paratext Lite's limitations, but as written it's confusing and could mislead learners about PTXPrint.
	**FLExTrans is thin.** The description mentions it integrates FLEx with Apertium, but a learner can't form a real picture of when to use it from that alone. How does it differ from Scripture Forge's AI drafting? Is it more appropriate for certain language families? Even one sentence on its typical use case would help.
	**The oral tools section is good**, but "Purpose" at the end reads like a heading that lost its content. It works as a summary line, but it's formatted inconsistently with the "Output" labels used elsewhere — worth standardising.
	---
	### Clarity & Language
	The writing is generally clear. A few specific notes:
	- **"Minutes from Paratext file to functioning Bible app"** (Scripture App Builder) is a strong, memorable claim — but it may set expectations that don't account for configuration, testing, or store submission. Worth a light qualifier.
	- **Scenario 3 in the Challenge** ("create a translation back into the consultant's language") correctly points toward Adapt It, but the scenario describes a *consultant checking* use case that Transcelerator also partially addresses. A learner who picks Transcelerator isn't wrong — the scenario may need tightening to make Adapt It the clearly intended answer.
	- The Transcelerator description — "if you've wondered how to translate all those English checking questions — this is your tool" — is a nice, conversational touch. It signals real-world empathy. More of that register in the drier sections would help.
	---
	### Practical Applicability
	The Challenge scenarios are well-designed for decision-making practice. One observation: **scenarios 1–7 are tool-identification tasks**, which is appropriate for a survey lesson. But the troubleshooting scenarios ask for something qualitatively different — they require learners to reason about *transitions* between tools and workflows. That's more sophisticated, and might benefit from a brief scaffold ("Consider: what data compatibility issues might arise?") rather than being dropped in cold.
	**The USFM troubleshooting scenario is particularly rich** and could almost be its own discussion. It surfaces a real and common problem in the field. If your colleague has time, this scenario deserves more than a quick debrief — it's worth learners discussing in pairs before sharing out.
	---
	### Summary of Priority Suggestions
	1. **Fix the PTXPrint formatting error** in the Paratext Lite section — this is the most urgent change.
	2. **Address the timing problem** — either trim Content significantly or split into two sessions.
	3. **Add a Key Takeaways section** after Content, probably grouping tools into 3–4 functional clusters rather than listing all 15+.
	4. **Consolidate the Change section** — one set of 3–4 questions with a transfer prompt, not two separate blocks.
	5. **Strengthen the Connect prompt** to create more specific motivation for this lesson's content.
	6. **Tighten Scenario 3** in the Challenge so the intended answer is unambiguous.
	7. **Expand FLExTrans** with a concrete use case or decision criterion.
	Overall this is a denser but in some ways more confident lesson than the literacy one — the tool descriptions have more texture and the Challenge is genuinely strong. The main structural issues are timing and the missing consolidation layer between Content and Challenge.

## Module 6: Grammar -done {toggle="true"}
	## Review of Module 6: Grammar Description and Documentation
	### What's Working Well
	This is a **tighter, more appropriately scoped lesson** than the previous two — four tools, sensible timing, and a Challenge that maps directly onto the Content. The case study is particularly good: asking learners to sequence tools into a workflow tests integrative understanding rather than just recall. That's the right level of cognitive demand for this material.
	The **"Purpose" line at the end of Content** — "Understanding natural ways of expression and documenting grammar for reference and training" — is actually one of the better conceptual anchors in the course so far. It tells learners *why* this matters, not just *what* the tools do.
	---
	### Learning Design & Structure
	**The Connect section is the weakest in the course so far.** It opens with a declarative statement ("Grammar descriptions are essential for training, reference, and preserving linguistic knowledge") and then asks a fairly abstract question ("How do grammar tools help linguists document language structure systematically?"). The problem is that learners can't meaningfully answer that question *before* they've learned the tools — so it functions more as a preview statement than a genuine activation of prior knowledge.
	A stronger Connect would tap into something learners already know from experience. For example: "Have you ever tried to explain a grammatical pattern in a language to someone who doesn't speak it? What made that hard?" That surfaces the real problem the tools solve, without requiring tool knowledge to answer.
	**Timing looks appropriate for once** — 5 + 25 + 20 + 10 lands at 60 minutes. Worth noting to your colleague that this is the first module in the three reviewed that fits the framework's target duration.
	**There's still no Key Takeaways section** at the end of Content. With only four tools it's less critical than in Module 5, but a two- or three-line summary clustering the tools by function (analysis → drafting → visualisation → publication) would still help learners organise before moving into Challenge.
	**The Change section is minimal.** Three reflection questions is thin for 10 minutes, and none of them include a transfer prompt. The third question — "What's the relationship between FLEx lexicon work and grammar description?" — is the most interesting one, but it introduces a connection (FLEx lexicon) that isn't explained in the Content section. A learner encountering FLEx for the first time in this module won't have the background to answer it well.
	---
	### Content Accuracy & Depth
	**The tool descriptions are functional but uneven.** PAWS and XLingPaper are described with enough specificity to be useful. FLEx and LingTree are thinner.
	The **FLEx description** is a particular concern. FLEx is one of the most powerful — and complex — tools in the SIL ecosystem, and it appears in multiple modules (it was mentioned in Module 5 in relation to FLExTrans). Describing it here only in terms of "text charting feature for discourse analysis" and "grammar sketches" understates its role significantly and may confuse learners who encountered it differently in Module 5. It's worth flagging to your colleague: is there a dedicated FLEx module elsewhere in the course? If not, this description is doing too little work.
	**LingTree** is described accurately but the entry "Easy graphic visualization of linguistic structures" is redundant with the line above it. One clean description would be tighter.
	**A small note on PAWS:** "\~100-page rough draft" is a memorable and useful concrete detail — that kind of specificity is exactly what helps learners calibrate expectations. More of that across all modules would be welcome.
	---
	### Clarity & Language
	The writing is clean and consistent. One observation: the module title says "Grammar description *and documentation*" but the Content doesn't distinguish between these two activities. Description (analysing and writing about grammar) and documentation (archiving and preserving linguistic knowledge) are related but not identical. If the module genuinely covers both, a sentence distinguishing them would help. If it's really just description, the title could be simplified.
	---
	### Practical Applicability
	**The Tool Matching exercise is well-constructed** — each tool maps to exactly one scenario, and the scenarios are distinct enough that learners can't guess by elimination too easily.
	**The Case Study has a gap, though.** The scenario describes a linguist with "3 years of data in notebooks and Word documents" — but the workflow question skips directly to "generating initial grammar draft" without addressing the *data entry problem*. How does that notebook data get into a form that PAWS or FLEx can work with? A learner thinking practically would immediately ask this, and the lesson doesn't give them an answer. Either the scenario needs adjusting, or a scaffolding question like "What would need to happen before you could use any of these tools?" would make the gap productive rather than frustrating.
	---
	### Cross-Module Observations
	Now that I've seen three modules, a couple of pattern-level observations worth raising with your colleague:
	**The video is doing unexamined heavy lifting.** Each module says "Play the video \[Topic\] Tools" but the written content essentially duplicates what a video would cover. It's worth asking: is the written Content meant to *replace* the video (as a text version for reference), *accompany* it (as notes), or *follow* it (as a debrief)? The relationship isn't defined, and that creates ambiguity for facilitators.
	**The Change sections across all three modules follow the same pattern** — a list of reflection questions with no transfer prompt or forward link. If this is consistent across all nine modules, it's a course-level design decision worth revisiting. A single action-oriented closing question per module ("What will you do differently, or try first, based on today's session?") would give the course a more intentional learning arc.
	---
	### Summary of Priority Suggestions
	1. **Rewrite the Connect prompt** to activate prior experience rather than preview content.
	2. **Address the FLEx description** — either expand it here or flag that it's covered in depth elsewhere, and resolve the cross-module consistency issue.
	3. **Add a scaffold question to the Case Study** that addresses the data migration problem before tool selection.
	4. **Add a Key Takeaways section** clustering the four tools by function.
	5. **Strengthen Change** with at least one transfer prompt and either explain or remove the FLEx lexicon question.
	6. **Clarify the video/text relationship** — this is a facilitation design question that affects all nine modules.

# Module 9: Quick Review -done {toggle="true"}
	### What's Working Well
	**This is the strongest module in the course.** The Connect scenario (Teams A, B, C) is the best opening in the entire course — concrete, immediately recognisable, and it activates real experience without requiring lesson knowledge to engage with. Your colleague should look at this Connect as a model for revising the weaker ones in earlier modules.
	**The city infrastructure metaphor** for the language development ecosystem is genuinely good. It gives learners a mental model that will outlast any specific tool detail.
	**The Key Takeaways section finally appears** — and it's well-written. Five clear, actionable statements that consolidate the whole course, not just this module.
	**The Change section is the best in the course.** The checkbox self-assessment, the concrete "This Week" action items, and the closing encouragement all work together. It closes the course with appropriate energy and clear forward momentum.
	### Priority Issues
	**The Content timing seems compressed.** 15 minutes for a module that synthesises 50+ tools, introduces a phased development framework, explains data flows, AND presents a 4-step troubleshooting methodology is very ambitious. Either the video carries significant weight here, or learners will feel rushed. This is the capstone — it probably warrants 25 minutes of Content, not 15.
	**The Tool Selection Framework heading is duplicated** — it appears twice consecutively in the Content section. Small fix but worth catching before the meeting.
	**"What type of issue does this likely is?"** — there's a grammatical error in Scenario 2 of the Challenge. Should read "What type of issue is this likely to be?"
	**The data flows section is the most technical content in the module** and sits without any scaffolding or example. For learners who've been absorbing tools across 8 modules, seeing USFM → LIFT → SFM laid out as pathways may click naturally — or may feel like new complexity arriving at the wrong moment. Worth asking your colleague whether this landed well in any pilot testing.
