Quote marks are probably the hardest punctuation mark to get right and use consistently. They’re difficult to get right partly because it’s easy to forget to put them in where they’re needed, partly because there are different levels of quotes, partly because there are different kinds of quotes, and partly because each language has their own way of handling what people say and how they say what someone else said. Are you confused yet? 🙂
Needless to say, there are many different ways to handle quotes and the marking of them. Here are a few ways...
(1) Perhaps the most common way is to use **double** quote marks for quotes and **single** quote marks for quotes inside of quotes.<br>*They discussed it among themselves and said, “ If we say, ‘ From heaven, ’ he will ask, ‘ Why didn’t you believe him? ’ But if we say, ‘ Of human origin, ’ all the people will stone us, because they are persuaded that John was a prophet . ” – Luke 20:5–6*
A third quote level – a quote inside a quote inside a quote – is sometimes marked with a colon along with (or instead of) an opening quote mark in an effort to help make the change in speech a bit more clear.<br>*Then the LORD said to Moses, “ Go to Pharaoh and say to him, ‘ This is what the LORD, the God of the Hebrews, says : “ Let my people go, so that they may worship me. … ” ’ ” – Exodus 9:1*
(2) Or they can be done the opposite way – using **single** quote marks for quotes and **double** quote marks for quotes inside of quotes…
*They discussed it among themselves and said, ‘ If we say, “ From heaven ”, he will ask, “ Why didn’t you believe him? ” But if we say, “ Of human origin ”, all the people will stone us, because they are persuaded that John was a prophet . ’ – Luke *20:5–6
(3) If quotes begin with a speech formula (i.e. a word or phrase that always appears at the beginning of a quote), then you could opt to use only closing quote marks, but not opening ones.

(4) You might not need to use quote marks at all, if the language uses a speech verb every time someone begins speaking and an affix and/or other marker that indicates when the person stops speaking.

(5) Another way to handle quotes is to mark different kinds of speech/quotes using different usfm codes in Paratext, which can then be formatted in different ways during typesetting.
For example...
```latex
\p Normal paragraph, first line indented
\m Normal paragraph, first line not indented 
\pm First quote level 
\pi2 Second quote level, quote within a quote 
\pmo Continuing first quote level, after the end of a quote within a quote 
\pi1 OT non-poetic quote (e.g. a quote from OT narrative material) 
\qc OT poetic quote (e.g. a quote from the psalms or one of the prophets)
```
