## Content
**Time:** 30 minutes
### Section 1: The Reality of Translation Software Support
When supporting translation teams, you'll encounter issues ranging from simple misunderstandings ("I can't find the button") to complex technical problems ("the synchronization is corrupted"). The challenge is that you often won't know everything about every tool, and that's okay.
**Key Point:** Effective support isn't about knowing all the answers immediately—it's about knowing how to systematically figure out what's wrong and where to find help when needed.
Your role combines three essential elements:
- Technical troubleshooting skills
- Understanding of translator workflows and context
- Clear, empathetic communication
### Section 2: The Systematic Troubleshooting Framework
When a translator reports a problem, use this framework to diagnose and resolve issues effectively:
**1. Clarify the Problem**
Before you can solve anything, you need to understand exactly what's happening. Panicked messages like "nothing works" or "it's broken" are starting points, not complete problem descriptions.
Ask clarifying questions:
- *What were you trying to do?* (Understanding their goal)
- *What happened instead?* (The actual behavior)
- *When did you first notice this?* (Timeline helps identify causes)
- *Has anything changed recently?* (Updates, new files, different location)
- *Can you show me exactly what you're seeing?* (Screenshots, error messages, specific behaviors)
<empty-block/>
<callout icon="/icons/drafts_yellow.svg" color="gray_bg">
	**Example:<br>**A translator says "Send/Receive isn't working." This could mean:
	- The button is grayed out (permissions issue)
	- It starts but never finishes (network/server problem)
	- It shows an error message (specific error to diagnose)
	- It completes but their changes aren't appearing (synchronization logic issue)
	- They can't find where to click (user interface familiarity)
	Each of these requires a different solution, so clarification is essential.
</callout>
**2. Understand the Context**
Translation work has patterns and contexts that affect troubleshooting:
- **Project structure:** Are they working with a team project or individual files? Is this a brand new project or one that's been running for years?
- **Collaboration setup:** Are multiple people working on the same data? How are they sharing files?
- **Work environment:** Are they in the field with limited connectivity, or in an office with stable internet? Are they using their own device or a shared computer?
- **Recent changes:** Did something just get updated? New operating system? New software version? Someone new joined the project?
Understanding context helps you ask the right questions and identify likely causes faster.
**3. Reproduce the Problem**
Whenever possible, try to see the problem yourself or have the translator show you exactly what's happening. This helps you:
- Verify what's actually occurring versus what they think is happening
- Notice details they might not mention
- Test potential solutions in real-time
Sometimes "it's not working" turns out to be "I'm clicking the wrong button" or "I'm looking in the wrong place." Screen sharing or working alongside them reveals this immediately.
**4. Test Systematically**
Rather than randomly trying things, work methodically:
- Start with the simplest, most common causes
- Change one thing at a time so you know what worked
- Document what you've tried and the results
- If something fixes the problem, make sure you understand *why* it fixed it
<callout icon="/icons/drafts_yellow.svg" color="gray_bg">
	For example, if a file won't open:
	1. Can they open other files? (Is it this specific file or all files?)
	2. Can someone else open this file? (Is it the file or their setup?)
	3. Does it open on a different computer? (Is it their device or the file?)
	4. Is there a backup they can try? (Can we work around the corrupted file?)
</callout>
**5. Know When to Escalate**
You won't solve every problem immediately. Learn to recognize when you need to:
- Consult someone more experienced
- Contact software vendor support
- Restore from backup and move forward
- Find a workaround rather than a complete fix
Knowing your limits and being willing to ask for help is a strength, not a weakness.
### Section 3: Supporting Translators Effectively
Technical skills matter, but so does how you interact with people who are often stressed, on deadlines, and worried about losing work.
**Acknowledge their stress:**
"I can hear this is really frustrating. Let's figure out what's happening together."
**Explain what you're doing:**
"I'm going to ask you some questions to help me understand exactly what's going on. Then we'll work through this systematically."
**Be honest about what you know and don't know:**
"I haven't seen this exact error before, but let me check a few things with you" is much better than pretending you know when you don't, or giving false reassurance.
**Manage expectations:**
If you need time to research, say so: "I want to investigate this properly rather than guess. Can I get back to you in two hours with a solution or at least a clearer picture of what's happening?"
**Document and share learning:**
When you solve a problem, document it. This helps you remember for next time and creates resources for the whole team. A simple document with "Problem / Symptoms / Solution" entries becomes invaluable.
### Section 4: Common Translation Software Issues
While every situation is unique, certain patterns appear frequently:
**Synchronization issues:**
- Often caused by network interruptions, conflicting changes, or corrupted project data
- Usually requires checking connection, reviewing conflict reports, or restoring from backup
**Missing work or data:**
- Could be user didn't save, file permissions changed, working in wrong location, or actual data loss
- Start by checking autosave locations, recent files, and backup systems
**Installation and compatibility:**
- Operating system updates can break software, antivirus can block features, insufficient permissions
- Check system requirements, update software, verify administrator rights
**Performance problems:**
- Large projects, insufficient memory, background processes competing for resources
- Investigate project size, available RAM, other running programs
**User interface confusion:**
- Person can't find a feature that exists, interface changed after update
- Often solved by screen sharing to see exactly what they're seeing
### Section 5: Building Your Support Toolkit
Effective support requires preparation:
**Knowledge resources:**
- Software documentation and help files
- User forums and community groups
- Vendor support contacts
- Internal team knowledge base
**Diagnostic tools:**
- Screen sharing software for remote support
- Log file analyzers when available
- Backup and recovery systems
- Test environments to replicate issues
**Communication tools:**
- Clear question templates for gathering information
- Screenshot annotation tools
- Step-by-step instruction formats
- Progress update templates for longer issues
**Personal habits:**
- Keep notes on issues you've solved
- Build relationships with experienced troubleshooters
- Stay current on software updates and known issues
- Practice explaining technical concepts in non-technical language

## Challenge
**Time:** 15 minutes
### Scenario 1: The Panicked Translator
You receive this message: "Everything is gone! I opened my project this morning and all my work from the past week has disappeared! What happened?!"
<callout icon="/icons/drafts_yellow.svg" color="gray_bg">
	**Your task:** Write out the first three questions you would ask to understand what's actually happening. Then identify what information you'd need before proposing any solutions.
</callout>
### Scenario 2: The Unclear Error
A translator sends you a screenshot of an error message in a language you don't speak well, and says "I keep getting this error and I don't know what to do."
<callout icon="/icons/drafts_yellow.svg" color="gray_bg">
	**Your task:** Describe your troubleshooting approach. What would you do first, second, and third? How would you gather information when you can't easily read the error?
</callout>
### Scenario 3: The Recurring Problem
A translator contacts you saying "This keeps happening. Every few days my work gets stuck and I have to restart the program. Can you fix it permanently?"
<callout icon="/icons/drafts_yellow.svg" color="gray_bg">
	**Your task:** How would you approach diagnosing an intermittent problem? What information would help you identify patterns? What would you tell the translator about your process?
</callout>
### For Mentors
As learners work through these scenarios, watch for:
- Are they jumping to solutions before understanding the problem?
- Do they ask clarifying questions before acting?
- Do they consider the translator's emotional state and stress level?
- Are they thinking systematically or randomly trying things?
Help them see that good troubleshooting is as much about asking the right questions as it is about technical knowledge.

### Looking Ahead
As you support translation teams:
- Remember that systematic troubleshooting is more valuable than memorizing every possible solution
- Your communication and empathy matter as much as your technical skills
- Building relationships with translators and other support people creates a stronger support network
- Documenting what you learn makes you and your whole team more effective over time
The goal isn't to become an expert in every translation tool—it's to become skilled at figuring out problems systematically and supporting people effectively while you do it.
